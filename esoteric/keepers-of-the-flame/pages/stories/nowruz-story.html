In the ancient times, on the night before Nowruz, Keepers would gather around the Sacred Fire with new wood gathered from the forest. As midnight approached‚Äîthe moment when one year ends and another begins‚Äîthe old ash would be carefully swept away. Then, as the first light of dawn touched the horizon, new kindling would be offered to the flame.

This ritual embodied a profound truth: renewal is not erasure, but evolution. The ashes of what was burnt are not wasted‚Äîthey become nutrients for the earth, nourishing new growth. The old flame does not die‚Äîit transforms, accepting new fuel and becoming even brighter.

In the Nowruz tradition, a table called Haft Sin (Seven S's) is prepared‚Äîeach item beginning with the letter S in Persian, each representing an aspect of the renewal:

Sabze (sprouted wheat) represents rebirth and renewal. Just as seeds sprout in spring, so too can the human soul sprout in new understanding. Samanu (sweet pudding) represents affluence and the rewards of perseverance. Senjed (jujubes) represent love and patience. Serkeh (vinegar) represents the test and trial‚Äîbitter experiences that teach wisdom. Somaq (sumac) represents the color of sunrise and the light that dispels darkness. Sekkeh (coins) represent prosperity and the material blessings that support spiritual work. S√≠r (garlic) represents health and protection.

Around these seven items, the Keepers traditionally place symbols of the Sacred Fire‚Äîa mirror representing truth and clarity, and a candle or small flame reflecting the eternal light of Ahura Mazda.

The table says: In renewal, we do not forget the lessons of struggle. In prosperity, we remain humble. In health, we serve others. In love, we find purpose. In the light of spring, we remember that even the deepest winter eventually yields to the returning sun.

‚úß The Spring Fire Festival ‚úß

In ancient times, as Nowruz approached, bonfires would be lit on hillsides across the land. These Chaharshanbe Suri fires (the Wednesday Fire Festival) became popular gatherings where Keepers and seekers would dance, sing, and purify themselves symbolically by jumping over the flames.

The jumping was not reckless but sacred‚Äîeach person who jumped the fire did so with a prayer in their heart: "Give me your bright redness, and take back my sickness." Not literally expecting sickness to vanish, but recognizing that in crossing from darkness to light, in moving toward the Fire, we release what no longer serves us and claim what we are truly meant to become.

The crowds would roar with joy as each person leaped. Young and old, noble and servant, rich and poor‚Äîall equal before the Fire. All participating in the renewal. All confirming that spring, and with it the possibility of transformation, comes to everyone.

For the modern Keeper of the Flame, Nowruz may not involve gatherings or bonfires. But the essential truth remains available to all who would access it: within you, in this very moment, the possibility of renewal exists.

What within you is ready to shed its winter covering and reveal new growth? What beliefs have you held that are no longer serving your journey toward truth? What new commitment to the Sacred Fire can you make, not once a year, but renewed every moment?

Nowruz teaches that the universe itself supports this renewal. The sun returns to the northern hemisphere. The earth warms and awakens. The days grow longer. Creation itself celebrates transformation. And the Keeper, in aligning with this cosmic movement, becomes a channel through which renewal flows into the world.

The Fire awaits those who would tend it. Spring awaits those who would step into it. And the eternal renewal of truth awaits those brave enough to be born again.
