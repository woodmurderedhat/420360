As the summer heat fades and the leaves begin their transformation from green to gold, the Keepers of the Flame gather to celebrate Mehregan‚Äîthe festival of Mehr, the deity of love, friendship, and the Sun itself, but also of contracts and the bonds that hold community together.

Mehregan falls in early autumn, at a time when the harvest has been gathered and there is leisure for community, for relationship, for remembering the connections that bind us. It is the festival that teaches that a Keeper cannot tend the Flame in isolation‚Äîwe are bound to each other by invisible threads of love, responsibility, and mutual support.

In traditional Mehregan celebrations, the Keepers would gather for a great feast. Unlike some celebrations that emphasize fasting or simplicity, Mehregan is a festival of abundance‚Äîfoods gathered from the summer's labor, wines made from the harvest, breads baked from new grain.

But the feast is never merely for the Keepers themselves. Part of the harvest is always set aside for the poor, the widow, the orphan, the stranger. Mehregan teaches that abundance is meaningful only when it is shared.

In this feast, ancient and social order hierarchy were temporarily dissolved. A shepherd sat next to a merchant. A servant ate at the same table as nobility. All were equal in the Keeper's circle, united by love for truth and for each other.

Mehregan was also the festival when old grievances were addressed and resolved. If two Keepers had been in conflict, Mehregan was the time to seek reconciliation. Not through forgetting the wrong, but through genuine understanding and the commitment to move forward together.

This practice reflected a deep truth about community: bonds broken through misunderstanding can be re-strengthened through honest dialogue. Mehregan taught that hurt feelings and conflict are not shameful‚Äîthey are inevitable in any community of truth-seekers. ‚úß The Light in Autumn ‚úß

As autumn progresses and the days grow noticeably shorter, Mehregan comes as a reminder: even as darkness approaches, there is light, warmth, gathering, and love. The Sacred Fire burns not brighter in autumn (the actual flames are the same), but its warmth becomes more precious because winter is coming.

This is perhaps the deepest teaching of Mehregan: love and community are not luxuries for times of abundance alone, but necessities for times of scarcity. The bonds we forge in the light of autumn must sustain us through the darkness of winter.

In the ancient mythology, Mehr is the archangel who presides over contracts and oaths. This is not accidental. For a Keeper to commit to another Keeper is to make an oath‚Äîto pledge that come what may, I will remember you are not alone, that I will support you, that our bond is sacred.

Mehr is sometimes depicted as warrior-like, with sharp eyes that can perceive through deception and treachery. This is because true love is not sentimental. It sees clearly. It does not excuse wrongs. But neither does it abandon. Mehr's love is the love that says: I see who you truly are‚Äîyour flaws and your virtues‚Äîand I love you completely.

Mehr is also the guardian of the sun‚Äîthe warmth that sustains all life, that rises each morning regardless of whether humans appreciate it, that gives freely to just and unjust alike. This is the model for a Keeper's love: given freely, not earned, maintaining its character regardless of response.

‚úß Modern Mehregan ‚úß

For the Keeper today, Mehregan may not involve elaborate feasts. But the essential practice is available: Gather with those you love. Share what you have. Address old hurts. Speak your love openly. Commit to supporting each other.

Look at the bonds in your life. Which ones need healing? Which ones are dying from neglect? Which ones are sustaining you? Where have you been withholding your love from fear or pride?

Mehregan teaches that the Sacred Fire is not tended by individual Keepers in isolation, but by communities of Keepers bound together by love. The greatest victory against the forces of darkness will not come through individual strength, but through communities of truth-seekers who have learned to truly love each other‚Äînot perfectly, but persistently, not easily, but deeply.

As autumn deepens and winter approaches, remember: you are not alone. There are others who tend the Flame. There are hands ready to help, hearts ready to care, voices ready to support. This is the gift Mehregan gives‚Äîthe reminder that love binds us all.
